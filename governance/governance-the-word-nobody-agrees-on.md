# Governance: The Word Everyone Uses and Nobody Agrees On

We're in a strange moment for governance.

The EU AI Act is moving into enforcement. Enterprises are racing to deploy generative AI while their compliance teams scramble to figure out what "responsible use" even means in practice.

And in the middle of all this, everyone keeps using the word "governance" as if we all agree on what it means.

We don't.

If I put your data team, your AI team, and your SAP team in a room and asked them all to define "governance," I'd get at least three different answers. Your data team would talk about quality, lineage, and stewardship. Your AI team would talk about fairness, model risk, and responsible use. Your SAP team would talk about master data workflows, golden records, and change approvals.

They're all right. And they're all incomplete.

Governance isn't a single discipline. It's a spectrum. And organizations that treat it as one fragmented program after another, each with its own framework, committee, and slide deck, keep reinventing the wheel.

Here's the position I want to stake out: the governance problem isn't a capability gap. It's a visibility gap. Most enterprises already have 70% of what they need. They just can't see how the pieces connect.

## Data Governance and AI Governance: Two Questions, One Continuum

Data governance answers a foundational question: can we trust our data?

That means defining ownership, measuring quality, controlling access, and tracking how data flows across systems. Most mature organizations have the building blocks: stewards, quality rules, audit trails, access controls.

AI governance picks up where data governance stops, but asks a different question: can we trust what the system does with the data?

This is the shift from managing information to managing decisions. AI governance introduces concerns that data governance was never designed to handle: bias testing, human-in-the-loop design, drift monitoring, and output validation. The accountability surface grows because the impact surface grows.

But here's what most organizations get wrong: they treat these as separate programs. The smarter approach is to recognize that most data governance capabilities are already AI governance capabilities, applied to a different lifecycle stage.

Data quality management builds trust in model performance. Lineage tracking helps surface bias. Access controls define boundaries for ethical use. Auditability enables defensible decision-making.

Same muscles. Different emphasis: inputs versus outputs.

## SAP MDG: The Operational Governance Layer Most AI Discussions Miss

SAP Master Data Governance is the operational execution layer. It's where governance policies become enforceable processes, embedded in the workflows your business runs on every day.

When someone wants to create a new vendor record, MDG defines who requests it, who reviews it, what quality checks it passes through, and who approves it before it reaches your production system. After a merger, MDG provides the framework to match, merge, and harmonize records at scale. These aren't administrative niceties. They're governance you can touch.

And here's the connection most people miss: SAP MDG is foundational AI governance that nobody labels as such.

A duplicate vendor record isn't just an ERP nuisance. It propagates into your analytics layer, your spend analysis models, and your AI-driven procurement recommendations. A miscategorized material doesn't just break reporting. It trains your demand forecasting model on flawed assumptions. When MDG enforces a validation rule or rejects a poorly formed record, it is protecting the integrity of the data that AI systems will eventually consume.

I've seen this disconnect firsthand. A company spends years implementing MDG with robust workflows, quality rules, and stewardship models. Then a separate team launches an AI governance initiative from a blank whiteboard, completely unaware that the operational backbone they need is already running. The disconnect is organizational, not technical. Bridging it is one of the highest-value, lowest-cost improvements most SAP customers can make.

## The Bottom Line

Governance is elusive because we treat it as a collection of separate problems. Data governance asks whether the data is correct. AI governance asks whether the outcome is acceptable. Master data governance asks whether the record is clean and approved.

These aren't competing questions. They're complementary layers of the same capability: making trustworthy, accountable, defensible decisions at scale.

The organizations that get this right won't have the fanciest frameworks. They'll be the ones that see governance as a single connected spectrum, recognize that most of the pieces are already in place, and have the clarity to wire them together.

Governance isn't a framework problem. It's a visibility problem. And for most SAP customers, the infrastructure to solve it is closer than they think.
